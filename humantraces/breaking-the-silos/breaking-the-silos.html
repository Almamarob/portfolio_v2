Breaking the Silos

Why data dies between the construction site and the boardroom — and how we can fix it.

The Core Problem

Data is born on construction sites and dies before it reaches investors. This isn't a technology problem. It's a structural one.

Real estate and construction technology exist in parallel universes. PropTech companies build tools for asset managers, portfolio optimizers, and tenant experience platforms. ConTech companies build tools for site logistics, workforce tracking, and material procurement. Both sectors generate enormous amounts of data. Neither talks to the other.

The result? Investors make decisions based on lagging indicators and spreadsheets. Developers manage risk with gut instinct and experience. Construction teams operate in data black holes, while the C-suite drowns in PowerPoint slides that barely reflect ground truth.

The Layer 0-3 Framework

To understand why this happens, you need to think in layers. I map the PropTech/ConTech ecosystem across four fundamental levels:

Layer 0: Physical Operations (The Construction Site)

This is where value is created and risk is born. Concrete gets poured. Delays happen. Costs overrun. Quality gets compromised or delivered. Everything that matters starts here.

Key players: ConTech companies (BIM tools, workforce management, logistics, IoT sensors, drones, computer vision for progress tracking).

The gap: Data generated here is fragmented, siloed in proprietary systems, and rarely structured for upstream consumption.

Layer 1: Asset Management (The Building as Product)

Once construction completes, the asset enters its operational life. PropTech dominates here: smart building platforms, energy management, tenant apps, maintenance scheduling.

Key players: PropTech companies (building management systems, IoT platforms, tenant experience tools).

The gap: These systems know what the building does now, but have no memory of how it was built. Construction data (actual costs, quality records, contractor performance) vanishes into filing cabinets and SharePoint folders.

Layer 2: Financial Layer (Capital Flows & Portfolio Management)

This is where investment decisions happen. Portfolio analytics, risk modeling, return projections, ESG compliance, debt structuring.

Key players: Financial PropTech (portfolio management platforms, valuation tools, ESG reporting frameworks).

The gap: Financial models rely on assumptions and comparables, not real-time construction or operational data. Risk assessments are backward-looking. Development timelines are guesses.

Layer 3: Strategic Intelligence (Market Analysis & Decision Support)

The advisory layer. Market research, feasibility studies, investment theses, site selection, competitive analysis.

Key players: Analysts, consultants, research teams, and increasingly, AI-powered market intelligence platforms.

The gap: Analysis is done with public data, broker reports, and backward-looking trends. The richest signal — what's actually happening on the ground — is invisible.

Why the Silos Exist

1. Different buyers, different budgets
ConTech is sold to construction managers and GCs. PropTech is sold to asset managers and landlords. Financial tools are sold to CFOs and investment committees. No single buyer controls the full stack.

2. Misaligned incentives
Construction teams are incentivized to finish on time and on budget, then move on. They have no reason to structure data for future asset managers or investors. Asset managers inherit buildings with zero context about how they were made.

3. No data standard
Every tool speaks its own language. BIM data doesn't flow into building management systems. Construction cost tracking doesn't feed into portfolio analytics. ESG reporting pulls from spreadsheets, not sensor data.

4. Time horizons don't match
Construction is a 12-24 month event. Asset management is a 10-30 year cycle. Financial exits happen in between. No one optimizes for the full lifecycle because no one owns it.

What Needs to Change

Fixing this isn't about building another dashboard or adding another integration. It requires rethinking how data flows through the value chain:

  • Layer 0 → Layer 1: Construction data must be structured for handoff. BIM models, progress tracking, cost actuals, and contractor performance records need to become part of the building's permanent record — not PDFs in a zip file.
  • Layer 1 → Layer 2: Operational performance metrics (energy use, maintenance frequency, tenant satisfaction) should feed directly into financial models and portfolio dashboards. Real-time building performance should inform refinancing decisions and asset valuations.
  • Layer 2 → Layer 3: Investment analytics should pull from live data, not quarterly reports. If a development is behind schedule, the financial model should update automatically. If a building's energy performance is deteriorating, the ESG score should reflect it immediately.
  • Layer 3 → Layer 0: Strategic intelligence should inform construction execution. Market insights, tenant demand signals, and portfolio-level learnings should loop back into how buildings are designed and built.

The Opportunity

The companies that bridge these layers won't be "PropTech" or "ConTech" — they'll be infrastructure. They'll build the pipes that connect site-level data to boardroom decisions. They'll make construction legible to finance, and finance legible to construction.

This is where the next generation of real estate technology gets built. Not in better dashboards. In better connective tissue.
